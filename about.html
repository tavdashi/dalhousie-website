About Dalhousie

History, geography, and the rhythm of the seasons in this Himalayan hill town.

A Town Shaped by the Hills

Dalhousie sits across five hills — Kathlog, Potreyn, Tehra, Bakrota, and Bald — in the Chamba district of Himachal Pradesh, at an elevation of roughly 1,970 metres above sea level. Founded in the mid-19th century as a summer retreat, the town still carries traces of its colonial past in its church spires, sloped-roof cottages, and quiet, winding lanes shaded by tall pines, deodars, and oaks.

History

Dalhousie was established around 1854 and named after Lord Dalhousie, a former Governor-General of British India. It quickly became a popular hill retreat thanks to its cool climate and sweeping views of the snow-capped Dhauladhar range. Many of the original colonial-era buildings, including churches and bungalows, still stand today and give the town its distinctive character.

Geography & Surroundings

The town overlooks the Ravi and Chenab river valleys, with the Pir Panjal and Dhauladhar mountain ranges visible on clear days. Dense forests of pine, deodar, and oak cover the surrounding slopes, making the area a haven for nature walks. Nearby Khajjiar, often called "Mini Switzerland," is a short drive away and adds a saucer-shaped meadow to the landscape.

Climate

Summers (April–June) are pleasantly cool, ranging from around 14°C to 24°C — a welcome escape from the heat of the plains. Monsoon season (July–September) brings lush greenery but also heavy rainfall and occasional landslides. Winters (November–February) are cold, often dropping below freezing, with snowfall common from late December onward.

Best Time to Visit

March to June is ideal for pleasant weather and blooming rhododendrons. September to November offers crisp air and clear mountain views after the monsoon. December to February suits travellers hoping to see snow, though some roads may be affected by snowfall.
